## KEY VERBATIM CLAIMS (no common cause / no g needed)

### 1. Abstract — the headline emergence-without-g claim (pp. 842)

> "In this article, a new explanation of the positive manifold based on a
> dynamical model is proposed, in which reciprocal causation or mutualism plays
> a central role. It is shown that the positive manifold emerges purely by
> positive beneficial interactions between cognitive processes during
> development. A single underlying g factor plays no role in the model."

*(Abstract, p. 842. This is the cleanest single statement: manifold "emerges
purely by positive beneficial interactions … A single underlying g factor plays
no role.")*

### 2. Introduction — mechanism gives the manifold but does not include g (p. 843)

> "This explanation identifies a plausible mechanism that gives rise to the
> positive manifold but that does not include g as a latent quantitative
> variable. At the very least, this demonstrates that a latent variable, which
> is well established psychometrically (i.e., in factor analyses), need not
> correspond to an actual quantitative variable, such as speed of processing or
> brain size."

*(Introduction, p. 843. Useful for the "strong first eigenvalue does not concede
a reflective common cause" point: a psychometrically solid latent factor
"need not correspond to an actual quantitative variable.")*

### 3. Model section — manifold does NOT require correlated resources/inputs (p. 845)

> "As we demonstrate below they need not be correlated to explain the positive
> manifold."

> "The positive manifold does not require correlated resources, although
> correlated resources may explain additional phenomena."

*(Model setup, p. 845. The planted-structure analogue: correlated common inputs
are not needed; the manifold comes from the interaction matrix M, i.e. from the
network of beneficial relations.)*

### 4. Discussion — initially uncorrelated processes, manifold emerges from interaction (p. 855)

> "The aim of the present article was to offer a new explanation for the positive
> manifold that does not include a g factor as an underlying quantitative
> dimension. In this explanation, we assume that in the initial phase of
> development, cognitive processes are uncorrelated. During development, the
> positive manifold emerges as a consequence of mutually beneficial interactions
> between these [processes]."

*(General discussion, pp. 855–856. Processes start uncorrelated; the manifold is
produced by the interactions, not by a shared cause.)*

### 5. Discussion — manifold ≠ single latent factor; g is not explanatory (pp. 855–856)

> "We have shown that positive manifold does not necessarily imply a single
> quantitative latent factor …"

> "… there is nothing wrong with using the g factor as a summary or psychometric
> index variable (e.g., in prediction), as long as we do not assume that this
> variable relates to a single underlying quantitative process or capacity. In
> this view, the g factor is not advanced as an explanatory variable."

*(General discussion, pp. 855–856. Directly supports: a g-like factor can be a
descriptive summary of correlated data without being a reflective common cause.
g "is not advanced as an explanatory variable.")*

## Notes / caveats for honest use

- Their term is "mutualism" (Lotka–Volterra with positive M_ij), framed as
  "reciprocal causation." This is a *network / interaction* account, the natural
  comparandum to a sparse pairwise network reading of a correlation matrix.
- They do NOT claim g is meaningless: a g factor remains fine as a *summary /
  index* variable (e.g. for prediction). The claim is specifically that the
  manifold does not *require*, and is not *explained by*, a single underlying
  reflective common cause.
- They explicitly note all such explanations (g, sampling, mutualism) are hard
  to falsify, and a full account may need elements of all three (p. 856). Don't
  overstate this as a refutation of g; it is an existence proof that a strong
  first factor is compatible with no common cause.
