### 1a. The five-property adjective battery (p1–p5), §8.4.1 "SG in the adjective class"

The criteria are stated as glosses attached to example (8), .md line 407:

> "(8) a happy woman (p1: attributive position) she is happy (p2: predicative position) very happy
> (p3: intensiWcation) happy/happier/happiest (p4: gradedness) unhappy (p5: un- preWxation)"

[de-mangled: p1 = attributive position; p2 = predicative position; p3 = intensification (very);
p4 = gradedness (comparative/superlative); p5 = un- prefixation.]

The class definition, .md lines 427–430:

> "Here happy, thin, alive, and utter can be assigned the label 'adjective', which as a class is
> characterized by syntactic properties fp1 ...p5g."
[de-mangled: "...characterized by syntactic properties {p1 ... p5}."]

The worked ranking, .md line 436:

> "characterized by p1---p5, thin is characterized by p1---p4, alive conforms to criteria p2, p3,
> and perhaps p4, while utter is characterized by p1 only. This makes happy a more prototypical
> adjective than thin, which in turn is more centrally adjectival than alive and utter."

On attributive position as a criterial property, and predicative-only adjectives, .md line 436 (cont.):

> "I must be taking the possibility of an element occurring in attributive position as a suYcient
> (i.e. criterial) property of adjectivehood, and that, therefore, the listed properties do not have
> equal status. ... The answer is that such words do exist, examples being adjectives that can only
> occur predicatively, which include many of those beginning in a- in English, e.g. alive. The word
> afraid is another example, cf. the man is afraid/*the afraid man."
[de-mangled: "suYcient" = sufficient.]

### 1b. The adjective-vs-preposition test battery, §8.4.4 — Huddleston & Pullum criteria as Aarts states them

Introduced at .md line 542:

> "We can approach Maling's data in a diVerent way, using the formalism introduced above, and making
> use of a number of tests introduced in Huddleston and Pullum et al. (2002: 606):"

The bulleted criteria, .md lines 544–574 (OCR splits each bullet across lines; reproduced as run-on):

> ".Prepositions but not adjectives can occur as head of a non-predicative adjunct in clause structure.10"

> ".AdjPs, other than those restricted to attributive or postpositive function, can mostly occur as
> complement to become; in general, PPs cannot."

> ".Central adjectives acceptveryandtooas degree modiWers; in general prepositions do not."
[de-mangled: "Central adjectives accept very and too as degree modifiers; in general prepositions do not."]

> ".Central adjectives have inXectional or analytic comparatives and superlatives; in general,
> prepositions do not.11"
[de-mangled: "...have inflectional or analytic comparatives and superlatives..."]

> ".Central prepositions license NP complements; in general adjectives do not."

> ".Central prepositions acceptrightandstraightas modiWers; adjectives do not."
[de-mangled: "Central prepositions accept right and straight as modifiers; adjectives do not."]

> ".Prepositions taking NP complements can normally be fronted along with their complement in relative
> and interrogative constructions, as in the knife [with which she cut it] or I don't know [to whom you
> are referring]; in general, adjectives cannot."

Aarts adds two further (enough) tests, .md lines 566–574:

> "We can add to this:
> .Central adjectives can be followed byenough:She is old enough to be your sister. (Maling 1983)
> .PPs can be preceded byenough, as Maling (1983) has noted: She is enough at ease to do the
> interview; APs generally cannot."
[de-mangled: "...can be followed by enough: She is old enough..."; "PPs can be preceded by enough..."]

The predicand requirement (footnote 10), .md lines 584–586:

> "Adjectives must pass the predicand requirement, which stipulates that they must be licensed by an
> overt or implied subject expression: 'Adjectives cannot head clause-initial phrases unless they are
> related to a predicand, whereas prepositions can' (Huddleston and Pullum et al. 2002: 530)."

### 2a. NEAR — section heading and thesis, §8.4.4 "IG between adjectives and prepositions: near and like"

Heading, .md line 526:

> "8.4.4 IG between adjectives and prepositions: near and like"

Thesis, .md line 529:

> "In this section I return to the element near, which we looked at in Chapter 6. We saw that for
> Maling (1983) near is classed as a transitive adjective, and is only superWcially a preposition. ...
> For Newmeyer (1998: 201f.) and for Huddleston and Pullum et al. (2002: 609) near belongs to both
> word classes, although for the latter its prepositional uses are more widespread. They note that
> near is 'highly exceptional in its syntax, combining a number of adjectival properties with those of
> the preposition' (2002: 609). I will also conclude that near as a lexeme belongs either to the class
> of adjectives or to the class of prepositions in any one conWguration, although not to both classes
> at the same time."

Maling's adjectival data for near, .md line 537:

> "She observes that in (25) near takes a comparative ending, while in (26) it modiWes a noun. In (27)
> enough must follow near—it cannot precede it—just as is the case with undoubted adjectives, cf. happy
> enough/*enough happy. The fact that near can also take NP complements, as in (28), is explained by
> surmising that nearisperhapsasurviving transitiveadjective(Maling1983:266)."
[de-mangled tail: "...near is perhaps a surviving transitive adjective (Maling 1983: 266)."]

Aarts's verdict on near in (28) "I saw him near the bar", .md line 621:

> "We Wnd that near is preposition-like with respect to most criteria in a simple sentence like (28).
> Disregarding the Wrst, inconclusive, criterion, we can say that p ¼ 7, x ¼ 4, and because x 6¼ (p
> x), that is, 4 6¼ (7 4), this is a case of strong convergence: near is a preposition converging on
> the class of adjectives."
[de-mangled: "p = 7, x = 4, and because x ≠ (p − x), that is, 4 ≠ (7 − 4)..."]

Attributive near (a near miss etc.), .md line 630:

> "Returning to Maling's data, sentence (26) involves a clear case of near occurring as an adjective:
> here the italicized items are without doubt adjectival by virtue of (1) their attributive position;
> (2) the possibility of modiWcation by an intensiWer like very; (3) the (possibility of a) comparative
> ending (the nearer shop); and (4) postmodiWcation by enough: a near enough guess. Notice that near
> cannot be preceded by typical prepositional modiWers such as straight or right."

Overall conclusion on near, .md line 634:

> "In conclusion, the data suggest that Maling was wrong in analysing near exclusively as a transitive
> adjective. In some syntactic contexts (most, in fact) it is a preposition, in others an adjective.
> Pace Maling, in the cases where it is followed by an NP, near is a preposition."

### 2b. LIKE — §8.4.4, examples (30)–(32), .md lines 638–648

> "(30) Jocelyn oVered a like suggestion.
> (31) Like his father, John had been called to give evidence.
> (32) John is becoming like his father."

> "In (30) like is an adjective occurring in attributive position, which can (possibly) be
> postmodiWed by enough (?a like enough suggestion). Notice, however, that it cannot be premodiWed by
> very or take a comparative form."

> "In (31) like would seem to be a preposition: it heads a non-predicative adjunct, it takes an NP
> complement, and it cannot be preceded by intensiWers or take a comparative form. If we apply the
> tests to (32), we Wnd that again they point to a predominantly prepositional analysis of like, pace
> Huddleston and Pullum et al. (2002: 608), who regard like as an adjective here. The adjectival
> properties of this word are the following: in addition to like his father being positioned after
> become, it can be preceded by very (very like his father) and it can take a comparative form (more
> like his father). However, the prepositional properties of like seem to outnumber the adjectival
> ones. Thus, like takes an NP complement, it can be premodiWed by an adverb (just like his father) or
> by enough (... he looks enough like his father ..., but not *He looks like enough his father), and
> fronting is just about possible (his father like whom he is becoming more and more)."

## TARGET 3 — Subsective Gradience (SG) vs Intersective Gradience (IG)
================================================================================

### 3a. Cleanest one-sentence prose distinction, Chapter 8 opening, .md line 280

> "Form classes can be subsectively gradient in displaying intra-categorial prototype eVects, such
> that we can speak of typical nouns, adjectives, and verbs, or they can be intersectively gradient in
> exhibiting convergence between categories."
[de-mangled: "intra-categorial prototype effects".]
==> This is the single best verbatim sentence mapping SG = within-category gradience, IG = between-category.

### 3b. The relation between SG and IG (formula), .md line 384

> "First, notice that there is an implicational relationship between SG and IG: with the exception of
> cases of 'true hybridity' (where x ¼ ðp xÞ ...), all cases of IG are ipso facto also cases of SG.
> ... Put formulaically, IG ¼ SGþstrong convergence."
[de-mangled: "(where x = (p − x) ...)"; "IG = SG + strong convergence".]

### 3c. SG does NOT mean degrees of class membership (key for mapping Aarts to graded-membership), .md line 350

> "Under the characterization of SG above, an adjective like thin is a more prototypical adjective
> than utter, because the former conforms to more adjectival properties than the latter ... It is
> important to stress that I am not claiming that there are degrees of class membership, as Zadeh has
> done: utter is as much an adjective as thin is; it is merely less prototypically a member of that
> class."

>>> CAUTION FOR THE PAPER: Aarts explicitly REJECTS graded (fuzzy/Zadeh) class membership. His SG is
graded PROTOTYPICALITY within a sharply-bounded class; his IG is property-set overlap, NOT membership
in two categories at once (see 3e). Mapping Aarts to "a graded-membership position" needs care: he is
a graded-prototypicality / sharp-boundary theorist, not a graded-membership theorist. He says so again
at .md line 282 and .md line 369.

### 3d. Formal definition of SG, §8.3.1, .md lines 338–346

> "Subsective Gradience If a,b 2 g, where g is a form class characterized by morphosyntactic
> properties fp1 ...png; and a is characterized by fp1 ...pxg, such that 0<x n; and b is
> characterized by fp1 ...pyg, such that 0<y<x; then a and b are in a subsective gradient
> relationship, such that a is a more prototypical member of g than b."
[de-mangled: "If a, b ∈ γ, where γ is a form class characterized by morphosyntactic properties
{p1 ... pn}; and a is characterized by {p1 ... px}, such that 0 < x ≤ n; and b is characterized by
{p1 ... py}, such that 0 < y < x; then a and b are in a subsective gradient relationship, such that a
is a more prototypical member of γ than b."]

### 3e. Formal definition of IG + the "intersection of properties, not categories" point, §8.3.2, .md lines 361–369

> "Intersective Gradience If a, b are form classes characterized by morphosyntactic properties
> fa1 ...amg and fb1 ...bng, respectively; and 9:, : a grammatical formative which conforms to a set
> of syntactic properties fc1 ...cpg, such that fc1 ...cxg [subset of] fa1 ...amg and fcxþ1 ...cpg
> [subset of] fb1 ...bng; then a and b are in an intersective gradient relationship with respect to :,
> and its projection :P."
[de-mangled: "...∃χ, χ a grammatical formative which conforms to a set of syntactic properties
{c1 ... cp}, such that {c1 ... cx} ⊆ {a1 ... am} and {c(x+1) ... cp} ⊆ {b1 ... bn}; then a and b are
in an intersective gradient relationship with respect to χ, and its projection χP." The OCR drops the
subset symbols, shown here as [subset of].]

> "Here the element : partakes of the categories a and b by displaying characteristics of both
> classes. The notion of intersection in the term 'Intersective Gradience' thus refers to the
> intersection of sets of properties, and not, crucially, to an intersection of the categories
> themselves. I thus exclude the possibility of a formative in a particular conWguration belonging to
> two form classes at the same time."

### 3f. Discreteness is logically prior to gradience, .md line 280 (cont.)

> "if it is claimed that there is gradience between two categories a and b, then it must Wrst be
> established that a and b actually exist as word classes. In other words, the well-motivated
> setting-up of discrete categories of form classes is logically prior to claiming that gradience
> obtains between them. Paradoxically, adherents of gradience models must face the fact that their
> analytical point of departure should always be rigid discreteness."

### 3g. "weak convergence" (SG) vs "strong convergence" (IG), .md line 352 and .md line 371

> "there are instances of SG—and utter is a case in point—where an element may bear a semantic
> resemblance to another word class. Thus, utter resembles adverbs in having an intensifying meaning.
> ... In Section 8.3.2 below I will refer to this property as weak convergence. Crucially, though,
> there are no syntactic points of overlap. The presence of the latter leads to what I will call
> strong convergence, a case of IG."

> "Where we have strong convergence (i.e. all the cases where x 6¼ (p x) in the formalization above),
> an element also displays one or more syntactic properties of another class. IG manifests itself
> through strong convergence."
[de-mangled: "...where x ≠ (p − x)..."]
