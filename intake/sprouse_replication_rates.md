## Paper A — Sprouse & Almeida (2012), Adger's *Core Syntax*

### 1. Headline replication rate(s) (verbatim + page)

**Abstract (p. 609):**
> "The results suggest that the maximum discrepancy between traditional methods
> and formal experimental methods is 2%. This suggests that even under the
> (likely unwarranted) assumption that the discrepant results are all false
> positives that have found their way into the syntactic literature due to the
> shortcomings of traditional methods, the minimum replication rate of these 469
> data points is 98%."

**General discussion (p. 630):**
> "Taken as a whole, these results suggest that the minimum replication rate for
> the data in Adger (2003) is 98%."

And, same page (p. 630):
> "It seems to us that the weakest possible conclusion is that there is a
> coherent set of 469 sentence types (forming 365 phenomena) that are at least
> 98% replicable."

**Conclusion (p. 633):**
> "The results suggest that there are 469 sentence types that form 365
> thematically coherent phenomena that are at least 98% replicable."

Per-analysis minimum replication rates (General discussion, p. 630):
- Standard frequentist tests: "out of 365 phenomena tested, 359 were clearly
  significant ... resulting in a replication rate of over 98%."
- Linear mixed-effects models: "we only tested 115 phenomena; however, 112 of
  those phenomena were clearly significant, for a replication rate of over 97%."
- Bayes factor analyses: "we tested 354 phenomena, and 347 were clearly
  significant, for a replication rate of 98%."

### 2. Sign-reversal / opposite-direction rate

**Zero sentences/phenomena replicated in the opposite (reversed) direction in
the main analyses.** In every results table the row "H1 in opposite direction
(Significant / Marginal)" has count 0:
- Table 2 (ME, frequentist, 115 phenomena, p. 619): opposite-direction = 0.
- Table 3 (ME, LME, 115 phenomena, p. 620): opposite-direction = 0.
- Table 4 (ME, Bayes factor, 104 phenomena, p. 621): opposite-direction = 0.
- Table 6 (yes–no, sign-tests, 250 phenomena, p. 624): opposite-direction = 0.
- Table 7 (yes–no, Bayes factor, 250 phenomena, p. 624): opposite-direction = 0.

(One *follow-up* re-test of a single existence item, the double-raising
"Sarah appears to have turned out to have left", went significantly in the
opposite direction: "11/39, p = .005 by sign-test", p. 629 ~-- but this is a
re-lexicalized follow-up, not one of the 365/469 main data points.)

Total negative (non-replicating) results across both experiments: "There were a
total of seven negative results from Experiments 1 and 2." (p. 629).

### 3. Data set / sample and N

- Source data set: David Adger (2003), *Core Syntax: A Minimalist Approach*,
  Oxford University Press.
- 873 data-like examples identified; after exclusions, **469 unique US-English
  data points** were tested, forming **365 statistical comparisons / phenomena**
  (p. 610, p. 614).
- 469 = 219 ME conditions (198 from Adger + 21 constructed controls) + 250
  yes–no existence tokens (p. 614).
- Participants: **440 naïve participants** total (abstract, p. 609): 240 in
  magnitude estimation (Exp. 1, p. 617) + 200 in yes–no (Exp. 2, p. 622),
  recruited on Amazon Mechanical Turk.
- Two tasks: magnitude estimation and yes–no. Three analysis types: standard
  frequentist, linear mixed-effects, Bayes factor (p. 609).

### 4. Full citation

Sprouse, Jon & Diogo Almeida. 2012. Assessing the reliability of textbook data
in syntax: Adger's *Core Syntax*. *Journal of Linguistics* 48(3) [Issue 03,
November 2012]. 609–652. DOI: 10.1017/S0022226712000011.
(Received 1 February 2011; revised 12 October 2011; published online 8 February
2012. Sprouse: UC Irvine; Almeida: Michigan State University.)

## Paper B — Sprouse, Schütze & Almeida (2013), random sample from *Linguistic Inquiry* 2001–2010

### 1. Headline convergence rate(s) (verbatim + page)

**Abstract (p. 219):**
> "We tested this sample with 936 naïve participants using three formal judgment
> tasks (magnitude estimation, 7-point Likert scale, and two-alternative
> forced-choice) and report five statistical analyses. The results suggest a
> convergence rate of 95% between informal and formal methods, with a margin of
> error of 5.3–5.8%."

**Introduction (p. 220):**
> "The results of the three tasks and five statistical analyses suggest
> convergence rates with the published informal judgments ranging between 86% and
> 99%, depending on the analysis."

**Section 4, The convergence rate (p. 229):**
> "The current study shows that the results of informal and formal methods are
> not identical, and suggests that the number of divergences is between 1% and
> 14% (±5.3–5.8%) of the phenomena published in *LI* between 2001 and 2010."

**Section 4.1, point estimate (p. 230):**
> "Therefore we believe that the most accurate convergence rate estimate between
> informal and formal methods for the syntactic phenomena explored in *LI*
> during the years 2001–2010 that can be derived from the results of this study
> is 95% ± 5.3–5.8%."

Range source (p. 229): "from 86% on the low end to 99% on the high end, with a
margin of error of 5.3–5.8% for each estimate."

**Per-task / per-analysis convergence rates — Table 6 (p. 229).** "Convergence
rates (in percentage) between each analysis and the informal results reported in
*Linguistic Inquiry* 2001–2010." (Slashed cells: left % assumes marginal results
non-significant; right % assumes marginal results significant.)

| Task | Directionality | One-tailed | Two-tailed | LME/ML | Bayes factor |
|------|---------------|-----------|-----------|--------|-------------|
| ME   | 99 | 93 | 92/93 | 86/88 | 89/90 |
| LS   | 97 | 93 | 93     | 88/90 | 89/90 |
| FC   | 97 | 94/95 | 94/95 | 95   | 94 |

### 2. Sign-reversal / opposite-direction rate

Counts of phenomena going in the **opposite (reversed) direction**, from the
descriptive directionality analysis ~-- Table 2 (p. 228), out of 148 pairwise
phenomena (296 sentence types):
- ME: predicted direction 146, **opposite direction 2**.
- LS: predicted direction 143, **opposite direction 5**.
- FC: predicted direction 144, **opposite direction 4**.

Statistically significant opposite-direction results (Tables 3–5, p. 228):
- ME (Table 3): "Significant in the opposite direction" = 2.
- LS (Table 4): "Significant in the opposite direction" = 2.
- FC (Table 5): "Significant in the opposite direction" = 3 (one-tailed) / 4 (ML).

### 3. Data set / sample and N

- Source population: standard acceptability-judgment data points published in
  *Linguistic Inquiry* 2001–2010. Estimated total US-English data points in *LI*
  2001–2010 ≈ **3635** (margin of error ≤ 6.9%, Table 1, p. 221); of these,
  estimated **1743** are standard acceptability judgments (p. 219, p. 221).
- Sample tested: "We tested 296 data points from the approximately 1743 English
  data points" (abstract, p. 219). Sampling drew **300 sentence types forming
  150 pairwise phenomena** (p. 222–223); after discovering 2 repeats, **148
  pairwise phenomena (296 sentence types)** were analyzed (p. 228).
- Margin of error on the convergence rate: **5.3–5.8%** (p. 223), reported as a
  range because of the bifurcated sampling (108 of 150 had published controls;
  42 controls were constructed).
- Participants: **936 naïve participants** total; "For each task we recruited 312
  participants on Amazon Mechanical Turk" (p. 225–226), i.e. 312 each for ME, LS,
  FC = 936; 104 ratings per condition per task (p. 227).
- Three tasks: magnitude estimation (ME), 7-point Likert scale (LS),
  two-alternative forced-choice (FC). Five analyses: descriptive directionality,
  one-tailed NHT, two-tailed NHT, mixed-effects (LME/ML), Bayes factor (p. 228).

### 4. Full citation

Sprouse, Jon, Carson T. Schütze & Diogo Almeida. 2013. A comparison of informal
and formal acceptability judgments using a random sample from *Linguistic
Inquiry* 2001–2010. *Lingua* 134. 219–248. DOI: 10.1016/j.lingua.2013.07.002.
(Received 17 January 2013; received in revised form 28 June 2013; accepted 1
July 2013; available online 1 September 2013. Sprouse: U Connecticut; Schütze:
UCLA; Almeida: NYU Abu Dhabi.)

## One-line takeaways (exact numbers, source-grounded)

- **A (Adger textbook):** minimum replication rate **98%**; maximum discrepancy
  **2%**; **469** data points / **365** phenomena; 440 participants; **0**
  reversed-direction results in the main analyses. (Sprouse & Almeida 2012,
  pp. 609, 630, 633.)
- **B (LI 2001–2010 random sample):** point-estimate convergence rate **95%
  (± 5.3–5.8%)**; range **86–99%** across analyses; divergences **1–14%**;
  **296** data points (148 pairwise phenomena) from ~1743; 936 participants;
  sign-reversals 2 (ME), 5 (LS), 4 (FC) out of 148. (Sprouse, Schütze & Almeida
  2013, pp. 219, 228, 229, 230.)
