## Target 1 — Which adjectives Maling treats as "transitive" / licensing an NP complement

**Definition of the term (p. 253):**

> "For example, as van Riemsdijk (1983) has amply documented, German has lots of
> what we might call 'transitive adjectives', i.e. adjectives which take
> case-marked NP-complements. So do Icelandic, Russian, Latin and many other
> case-marking languages." (p. 253)

> "I use the term 'transitive adjective' advisedly, to indicate only that the
> adjective is directly followed by a NP complement; in fact, the diachronic
> developments reported here suggest that the NP-complements to adjectives are
> usually not grammatical 'objects' but rather 'oblique objects' (cf. Bresnan
> (1981))." (pp. 253-254)

**The three English items she actually examines (p. 254):**

> "In this paper, I will discuss the synchronic status of three lexical items,
> *like, worth* and *near*, which were all unambiguously adjectives in Old
> English. These have all been analyzed as prepositions in modern English by at
> least some grammarians, e.g., Quirk *et al.* (1972), but some or all of them
> have been considered adjectives by other linguists, e.g. Bresnan (1978, fn. 2)
> and Lightfoot (1980)." (p. 254)

NOTE on the rest of the prompt's candidate list (due, similar, opposite): Maling
does **NOT** present a list including *due*, *similar*, or *opposite* as English
transitive adjectives. Her English discussion is confined to *like*, *worth*, and
*near*. The only other "transitive adjective" she names is the Norwegian/Germanic
data: *afraid (of)* (Norwegian *redd*), and in note 16 the four Norwegian
NP-taking adjectives:

> "Lars Hellan informs me that there are four adjectives in Norwegian which can
> take direct NP-complements: redd 'afraid', lik 'like', naer 'near', and verd
> 'worth'." (p. 283, note 16)

So for *due / similar / opposite* as Maling-listed transitive adjectives:
**NOT FOUND.**

## Target 2 — Degree modification by "well" (e.g. *well worth*) as a preposition-like diagnostic

**NOT FOUND.** Maling does not use *well* (as in *well worth*) as a diagnostic
anywhere in the paper. Her degree-modifier diagnostics are *very*, *much*,

> "On the other hand, unlike adjectives, metaphorical PPs also allow *much*, as

But *well worth* specifically: **NOT FOUND.**

## Target 4 — Inflectional comparison of "near" (nearer / nearest) as evidence of categorial status

**FOUND (p. 270):**

> "It takes a synthetic comparative and superlative, and *enough* follows the
> head rather than preceding it, as it would if *near* were a preposition." (p. 270)

with examples (39):

> "(39) a. Kim put the lamp nearer (to) the bed.
>       b. Don't go any {nearer / *more near} (to) the water!" (p. 270)

**Prenominal/attributive uses of *near* (also adjectival evidence), examples (41), p. 270:**

> "Moreover, there exist attributive prenominal uses of *near*:
> (41) a. the near shore.
>       b. a near miss.
>       c. Take the nearest one to you.
>       d. They remained near strangers after (nearly) twenty years of marriage." (p. 270)

**Summary-table corroboration (p. 273, table (45) "Diagnostic Criteria for A versus P"):**
*near* is marked "+" for "has synthetic comparative" (alongside A "+", P "-",
*like* "-", *worth* "-"). In the surrounding prose Maling notes:

> "By comparison the relation between *near* and *nearer* is clearly productive." (p. 273)

(contrasted with *elder*/*older*, where *elder* is a non-productive relic).

## Target 6 — Maling's OVERALL conclusion on the categorial status of *worth* and of *near* (and whether reanalysis A→P is complete)

### Section 2 framing statement (p. 266):

> "In this section we will show that *like* and *worth* are best analyzed as
> prepositions, whereas *near* passes all the tests for adjectivehood, making it
> perhaps the only surviving relic of the class of transitive adjectives." (p. 266)

### On *worth* — verbatim conclusions:

Opening of §2.2 (p. 268):

> "As counterintuitive as it may at first appear, *worth* is best analysed as a
> preposition. It certainly passes the diagnostic tests for preposition as well
> as *like* does, even better in fact, since there is no *unworth* with a
> presumably adjectival base." (p. 268)

> "I conclude that analyzing *worth* as a P is more a problem for the linguist's
> conscious mind than for his unconscious." (p. 268)

Closing of §2.2 (p. 269):

> "Thus it seems reasonable to conclude that *worth* is a perfectly well-behaved
> preposition in modern English." (p. 269)

### On *near* — verbatim conclusions:

Opening of §2.3 (p. 270):

> "If *worth* is usually misanalyzed as an adjective, *near* on the other hand
> is usually misclassified as a preposition." (p. 270)

> "Closer examination, however, shows that *near* is best analyzed as an
> adjective." (p. 270)

> "Even when *near* is used locatively, it behaves syntactically like an
> adjective." (p. 270)

Closing reasoning of §2.3 (p. 271):

> "Therefore *near* must be the head in (43b), and instead of the phrase
> structure in (42), we must be dealing with a locative AP." (p. 271)

> "The fact that these phrases are locatives accounts for their use with verbs
> like *put*, while the fact that they are syntactically APs accounts for the
> existence of the synthetic comparatives and the position of *enough* with
> respect to the head." (p. 271)

### Whether the reanalysis is COMPLETE (A → P): YES for *like* and *worth*; NO for *near*.

The paper's title is "A Case of Categorial Reanalysis," and the formal
Conclusion (§4, p. 278) states the reanalysis happened (past tense, completed)
for *like* and *worth* only:

> "In this paper I have argued for the categorial reanalysis of two transitive
> adjectives in the history of English; in so doing, I have had to examine the
> various criteria used to identify syntactic categories." (p. 278, §4 Conclusion)

That the reanalysis is treated as **completed** (not in progress) is explicit at
p. 277:

> "It is obvious that a categorial reanalysis has since taken place. I have not
> yet had time to try to pinpoint just when this took place, or to determine
> whether the changes in use took place rather quickly, or only gradually over
> the intervening centuries." (p. 277)

And on *like*/*worth* being forced out of their adjectival uses and replaced
(p. 278):

> "Once *like* and *worth* were identified as prepositions rather than
> adjectives in English, then all their clearly adjectival uses, as defined by
> the phrase-structure rules in (55), were forced out and replaced by related
> forms." (p. 278)

> "*Like* was replaced by *likely* and *alike* in its two intransitive uses, and
> *worth* was replaced by *worthy* both intransitively and prenominally." (p. 278)

By contrast, *near* is explicitly **NOT** reanalyzed; it remains an adjective and
is the "only surviving relic" of the transitive-adjective class (p. 266, quoted
above). The summary table (45), p. 273, scores *near* as "+" on the adjectival
criteria (synthetic comparative, prenominal position, occurs with postmodifier
*enough*, degree modification by *very*/*how*/*so*/*too*), parallel to A; while
*worth* and *like* pattern with P.

### Section 2.4 summary-prose note on *near* (p. 273):

> "Remember that only locatives or directionals behave like 'typical' Ps with
> respect to those tests that I have argued to be semantically or functionally
> based." (p. 273)

## Bonus (likely useful for the diagnostic-validity paper): the *enough*-test as the one strictly syntactic A/P diagnostic

Maling's central methodological finding is that most traditional A/P diagnostics
(subcategorization, coordination, degree modifiers) cut across categories, and
that the position of *enough* relative to the head is the one diagnostic that is
"purely syntactic":

> "Clearly the rule of *Enough*-shift blocks if the head is a preposition. Here
> at last we have a criterion that seems to be purely syntactic; in English,
> *enough* follows adjectives, but precedes prepositions." (p. 263)

> "The conclusion is that *enough* follows the head of the phrase it modifies
> unless prohibited from doing so by (23)." (p. 265)

> "This turns out to be an extremely useful test." (p. 265)

> "Only the position of *enough* relative to the head of a phrase turned out to
> be usable in distinguishing adjective from preposition." (p. 265)
