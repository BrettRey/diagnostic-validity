## 1. ATTRIBUTIVE position — Matthews's characterization (pre-head, as a property/diagnostic of adjectives)

**The core definition of the attributive position (Ch. 1, §1.8, p. 10):**

> "The 'attributive position', as current grammars define it, is that of an
> adjective in a noun phrase that both modifies and comes before a head noun."
> — p. 10 (§1.8)

**The function-pair characterization of an adjective (Ch. 1, §1.1, p. 2):**

> "In both positions tall is the same part of speech. It can be characterized
> accordingly by two main functions, one predicative and one attributive: one in
> the structure of a clause or sentence, and the other within the structure of
> phrases." — p. 2 (§1.1)

> "The second function is performed by adjectives in another 'position', which is
> traditionally 'attributive'. In the tall chief, the function of tall is as a
> unit attributive to, or as a modifier of, a noun." — p. 2 (§1.1)

**The compact statement of what "attributive" means (Ch. 1, §1.2, p. 3):**

> "By the attributive position we mean that of a premodifier: of tall in the tall
> chief, or of good in a good dinner. An adjective that is not a premodifier is
> predicative". — p. 3 (§1.2)

**Attributive as the *most distinctive* diagnostic for the class (Ch. 2, §2.6, p. 23):**

> "But the crucial function would be that of a premodifier, or a modifier in the
> attributive position; not, in the way in which it is possible to read a
> traditional definition, of a modifier which is either attributive or
> postpositive." — p. 23 (§2.6)

> "They are exceptions, in the received view, in that while they are adjectives,
> and have meanings characteristic of that class, they do not have the attributive
> function, by which adjectives are most clearly distinguished." — p. 23 (§2.7)

## 2. POSTPOSITIVE position — Matthews's characterization

**The three-position framing and the postpositive as a third "main function" (Ch. 1, §1.8, p. 10):**

> "In current grammars this is presented simply as a third function. We may
> therefore speak, as Quirk and his colleagues do speak, of 'three positions of
> adjectives': the attributive, the predicative, and the 'postpositive' (1985:
> 418). For Huddleston and Pullum, the postpositive is likewise one of three 'main
> functions' (2002: 528), separate from both the others. An adjective with this
> function 'commonly occurs' after units such as something or anyone, and 'under
> restricted conditions they occur' in phrases headed by nouns." — p. 10 (§1.8)

**Postposition vs. attribution at the level of order vs. construction (Ch. 1, §1.8, p. 10):**

> "By a 'position' we might mean, literally, a place in a sequence. A postmodifier
> has in that sense a position different from that of a premodifier." — p. 10 (§1.8)

**The reduced-relative-clause analysis (Ch. 1, §1.9, p. 12):**

> "It is therefore possible to say, with Quirk and his colleagues, that 'a
> postpositive adjective . . . can usually be regarded as a reduced relative
> clause' (1985: 418)." — p. 12 (§1.9)

> "As the postpositive position is more, however, than a mere variant of the
> attributive, it is also more than yet another extension of the predicative." —
> p. 12 (§1.9)

**Postpositive-only adjectives (e.g. *proper*, *elect*, *designate*, *galore*) (Ch. 1, §1.9–1.10, pp. 12–13):**

> "For there is also a small class of adjectives, or of words traditionally
> classed as adjectives, that are either restricted to this position or are
> restricted, again, in certain senses." — p. 12 (§1.9)

> "Within that class there is, however, a subclass, with members such as elect or
> proper in the city proper, which are 'postpositive only'." — p. 13 (§1.10)

**The "options available" / "people present" type (postmodifier with a following dependent or contrastive sense):**

Matthews discusses this type repeatedly. On *present*/*available*-style postmodifiers
and the "movements possible" alternation (Ch. 4, §4.4, p. 54):

> "But while one can also say the movements possible, the difference in the place
> of the adjective is overshadowed by a far more general and more regular
> opposition, in which the movements possible, on the one hand, contrasts with the
> movements possible as, on the other hand, the possible movements contrasts with
> the possible movements." — p. 54 (§4.4)

On *present* specifically (the *parliamentarians present* type, contrasting with
attributive *present*) (Ch. 4, §4.4, pp. 53–54):

> "The present parliamentarians, for example, would refer to people who are the
> current members of a parliament, and is distinguished by this meaning from the
> parliamentarians present. Its sense in the latter is in turn as in The
> parliamentarians were present." — p. 54 (§4.4)

In Ch. 10 (the late chapter on modifiers as postmodifiers), Matthews ties
postposition to adjective phrases that carry their own post-head dependents (§10.1,
p. 159 area):

> "Other adjective phrases, which by implication have dependents following the
> adjective, 'occur postpositively'." — p. 159 (§10.1; quoting Payne & Huddleston)

## 3. THE KEY CLAIM TO TEST — did Matthews say the positional *diagnostics* for the adjective class have DRIFTED / been quietly reweighted over time as inconvenient results accumulated?

### VERDICT: The claim as our paper frames it does NOT hold up in Matthews (2014). It needs softening / re-attribution.

Matthews does use the word **"drift"**, and prominently — but he uses it in Sapir's
sense to describe how the **English language itself** has changed (the attributive
and predicative *uses* of adjectives have diverged historically). He does **not**
claim that **linguists' positional tests for the adjective class** were quietly
reweighted, loosened, or shifted over time to absorb inconvenient results. That is
a different (methodological / sociology-of-the-discipline) claim, and Matthews does
not make it.

**What "drift" actually refers to in Matthews — the LANGUAGE, not the diagnostics (Ch. 11 Envoi, §11.1, p. 173):**

> "If this study has a general conclusion it is one that has been clear from the
> beginning: that the attributive and predicative uses of adjectives have
> diverged. Their divergence reflects changes in English over the Modern period
> especially, many dating back to the end of the Middle Ages. The drift, in the
> sense of Sapir (1921), continues." — p. 173 (§11.1)

> "The divergence is in both syntax and the lexicon. An adjective in one position
> is nevertheless the same part of speech, or in another term a member of the same
> syntactic category, as in any other." — p. 173 (§11.1)

### What Matthews DOES say about the (in)stability / reliability of positional criteria

He says something *adjacent* but importantly weaker than our claim: that linguists
historically **abandoned necessary-and-sufficient criteria** for parts of speech in
favour of a looser "cluster of properties" characterization of *central* members,
and that the criteria are messy, boundary-fuzzy, and partly retained to keep the
traditional category intact. This is a one-time *historical retreat* in method (early
20th c.), described approvingly-ish, not an accusation of ongoing opportunistic
reweighting.

**The retreat from necessary-and-sufficient criteria to a "cluster of syntactic properties" (Ch. 2, §2.5, p. 21) — the single strongest passage:**

> "Such niggling is notorious, and was the basis on which definitions of the parts
> of speech were rubbished in the first half of the twentieth century. Most
> linguists have accordingly retreated from the attempt to give both necessary and
> sufficient criteria. A 'cluster of syntactic properties' (Huddleston and Pullum
> 2002: 528) can be said to characterize the 'central' members of a part of
> speech." — p. 21 (§2.5)

> "In their analysis, a central adjective has positions which include the
> 'postpositive'; is gradable and takes 'degree modifiers' such as very; and
> 'characteristically' takes other adverbs. It is in the light of this that other
> adjectives, which are not central, are delimited." — pp. 21–22 (§2.5)

**The closest Matthews comes to a "tests kept only to save the category" suggestion (Ch. 2, §2.5–2.6, p. 22):**

> "This brings us back, however, to the question implied at the beginning of this
> chapter, as to whether we have good reason to include them in this category,
> other than to ensure that somehow, somewhere, they will fit into the traditional
> scheme." — p. 22 (§2.5)

Note this is a question about *category membership* (why lump heterogeneous words
together), not a claim that the *positional diagnostics themselves* were reweighted
over time.

**On the unreliability of specific positional/category judgements (Ch. 2, §2.7, p. 24):**

> "While this criterion, however, may seem natural enough, the evidence may be less
> than decisive." — p. 24 (§2.7)

> "Such judgements are at the limit, however, of those that can be made reliably."
> — p. 24 (§2.7)

> "One conclusion might be that the boundary may be 'fuzzy'. But on what evidence
> is there a boundary at all?" — p. 24 (§2.7)

**That criteria are abstractions to be applied "with circumspection" (Ch. 11 Envoi, §11.4, pp. 177–178):**

> "The structure of a language is at every level an abstraction, and the criteria
> for abstraction, especially at the level at which constructions are established,
> are such that it is hard to separate what is directly open to empirical argument
> and what will not be. Criteria must therefore be applied with circumspection."
> — pp. 177–178 (§11.4)

**On gradience/fuzziness as a long-standing recognition, not a recent reweighting (Ch. 2, §2.1, p. 16):**

> "Such insights have been familiar since the 1960s, and in part earlier. In
> proposing a system of five classes, Jespersen acknowledged that 'we are unable
> to define such categories so rigidly as to be left with no doubtful or
> borderline cases' (1924: 92)." — p. 16 (§2.1)

## Bottom line for the paper

- Targets 1 and 2 are solid and well-supported with verbatim, page-cited quotes.
- Target 3, as our paper currently frames it (positional **diagnostics** quietly
  reweighted over time as inconvenient results accumulated), is **not** what
  Matthews argues. Matthews's "drift" is Sapirian drift of the **language**.
- If the paper wants Matthews as support, the defensible attribution is narrower:
  he documents (a) a historical, one-time disciplinary retreat from
  necessary-and-sufficient criteria to a looser "cluster of properties" for
  *central* members (p. 21), and (b) that positional criteria yield fuzzy,
  sometimes-undecidable boundaries and "evidence [that] may be less than decisive"
  (p. 24). Neither is the same as "the tests that once defined the class were
  quietly shifted to protect it."
- Recommend a ⟨VERIFY⟩ flag on any sentence in our draft that attributes the
  diagnostic-drift / reweighting claim to Matthews 2014, and rewording to the
  narrower, source-grounded version above.
